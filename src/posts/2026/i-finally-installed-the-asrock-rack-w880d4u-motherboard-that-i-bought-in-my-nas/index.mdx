I bought an
[ASRock Rack W880D4U motherboard](https://www.asrockrack.com/general/productdetail.asp?Model=W880D4U)
(along with an Intel Core Ultra 5 245K CPU) back in November to upgrade my
self-built NAS. And they’ve been sitting in their boxes since because I had no
DDR5 ECC RAM to go along with them.

## Getting RAM, slowly

I had ordered two sticks of Kingston 48GB 5600MT/s DDR5 ECC RAM from Amazon at
the palatable price of £230 each. Sadly, they never materialised – Amazon
cancelled my order after six months due to ‘a lack of availability’. The same
RAM now costs £1,573.60 a stick on one UK site selling it and £2,262.36 a stick
on the only other UK site I can find.

I got on the chat with Amazon and asked if there was anything they could do
(mentioning the fact that the price of the RAM had increased significantly).
They said they would offer a ‘price honor’ (their spelling) and, if I bought the
same item from another seller on Amazon.co.uk, they would refund the difference.

The first problem with that is there aren’t any sellers offering the same RAM on
Amazon.co.uk. The second problem is that, even if there were, I’d be asking for
a refund close to £4,000. Even if, by some miracle, Amazon gave me that refund,
I’d be worrying about my Amazon account being flagged as unprofitable and then
being closed.

Begrudgingly, I let my failed Amazon order go and kept an eye on the second-hand
market. That paid off as I was able to get two sticks of used Hynix 16GB DDR5
ECC RAM at the non-silly price of £160 in total. (I was originally going for
96GB of RAM in total, but 32GB will easily do for now.)

### Why did I pick this board?

This model might seem a bit obscure, but it’s exactly what I wanted for my use
case of a NAS (running
[TrueNAS Community Edition](https://www.truenas.com/truenas-community-edition-features/)).

First, being based on the Intel W880 chipset, it supports
[ECC RAM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ECC_memory) with inexpensive consumer
CPUs like the
[Intel Core Ultra 5 245K](https://www.intel.com/content/www/us/en/products/sku/241067/intel-core-ultra-5-processor-245k-24m-cache-up-to-5-20-ghz/specifications.html)
that I bought.

Second, it has a baseboard management controller (BMC) and supports the
[Intelligent Platform Management Interface (IPMI)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intelligent_Platform_Management_Interface).
As part of that, it has a web interface that includes remote desktop-like
functionality. That means I don’t need to connect a keyboard, mouse or monitor
to the machine – I can instead connect an Ethernet cable from my router to a
dedicated port on the motherboard, visit an IP address in a web browser and do
any configuration or troubleshooting conveniently from there.

The board is also in the microATX form factor (which fits in my small case) and
supports connecting eight SATA drives (four via dedicated ports, four via a
supplied mini-SAS to 4× SATA cable). It also isn’t an obscene price.

Unfortunately, it’s not readily available in the UK. It can be easily imported
via Amazon (on the UK site) or Newegg though.

## My existing kit

I was planning on keeping the rest of my existing kit. That is a Fractal Design
Node 804 microATX case, a Corsair SF450 Platinum PSU, an AQC107-based 10 GbE
PCIe card and, of course, my existing drives.

## Installing the board

### Hooking up some power

I was upgrading from an older ASRock Rack C246-based Mini-ITX board. The old
board had one ATX 12V power input while the new one has three. The manual for
the new board wasn’t very clear about whether all three needed to be connected,
so I emailed ASRock Rack to ask. They said:

> This depends on the hardware you are going to use on the board. If it’s
> minimal then you can use the Atx12v4 ( 4 pin) and the Atx 12 v1 & 12 v2. If
> you planning on using a lot of hardware in the future maybe an idea to invest
> in ta psu with 3 atx12v connectors.

(Note that the ‘Atx12v4’ mentioned in that quote is actually called ‘ATX4PIN1’
in the manual – it’s a 4-pin Micro-Fit socket that you connect the 24-pin ATX
cable to via a supplied adaptor.)

I wasn’t going to be connecting much to the board, so I took from their reply
that I would be fine connecting only two ATX 12V cables. My modular PSU, a
now-discontinued Corsair SF450, only came with one ATX 12V cable. That cable
plugs into the same sockets on the PSU as the 8-pin PCIe power cables do, and
there were three such sockets on the PSU in total. I wasn’t using the PCIe
cables, so I just ordered another Corsair ATX 12V cable. That turned out to be a
two-pack, so I now had cables for all three ATX 12V sockets on the motherboard.

It turned out, though, that that wasn’t my only PSU cable-related problem. My
PSU uses the compact SFX form factor and came with short cables. The layout of
the new motherboard was different from my old one, and the 24-pin ATX and 8-pin
ATX 12V cables that my PSU came with barely reached their new destinations. The
standalone cables that Corsair sells are normal-length ones (i.e. longer than
the ones bundled with my PSU), so I just ordered more to replace those two
cables as well.

### A few other installation snags

I was upgrading from a Mini-ITX motherboard, and so I hadn’t installed the
additional microATX stand-offs in my case. Hence, I needed to put those in. It’s
been a while since I’ve had to screw motherboard stand-offs in myself as the
last few desktop cases I’ve bought have had them pre-installed. I couldn’t find
the tool that came with the case to tighten them, so I first did it by hand. And
when I then went to install the motherboard, it didn’t seem to be lying flat (my
clue was when one of the screws didn’t reach the stand-off underneath). As it
turned out, I hadn’t screwed all the stand-offs in fully, so I had to remove the
board and sort that before reinstalling the board.

When I went to install my 10 GbE PCIe card, it wasn’t correctly lined up
horizontally with the slot on the motherboard. I don’t recall having had a
similar problem before. I had to loosen the motherboard screws, shift the
motherboard 1mm to the right and install the card before tightening the screws
again.

I had bought a Noctua NH-D9L CPU cooler to use with my new set-up. When I was
getting ready to install everything, I got slightly worried that the cooler
wouldn’t be compatible with the motherboard. So, I checked the NH-D9L
compatibility page on Noctua’s website. To my relief, the motherboard was one of
two ASRock Rack LGA1851 models listed as compatible. And, indeed, I was able to
install the cooler fine.

I also decided to replace all the fans that came with the case as, rather than
being PWM fans, they connected to a fan controller. That fan controller had a
short SATA power connector, which is annoying to connect up. So I ditched those
fans and the fan controller and installed some better Noctua PWM fans.

I’ll also mention that I first installed the new Noctua front fan using the
bundled NA-AV2 double-sided rubber mounts – and then found that they prevented
the front dust filter from being reinserted (at least without cutting the rubber
mounts with scissors). I ended up just using screws instead.

## Booting up the board

As I’d replaced almost every PSU cable, I disconnected anything not absolutely
necessary for my first attempt at powering on the motherboard just in case I
ended up frying something. It powered up fine, so I ran a single pass of
MemTest86 as a quick check that my second-hand RAM was working. That passed, so
I then updated the BMC firmware and the BIOS via the BMC web interface. (The
board came with BIOS version 1.05 and BMC version 1.07.00, which were oddly
older than the ‘first version[s]’ listed on ASRock Rack’s website. I’ve updated
to BIOS version 2.03 beta and BMC firmware 1.10.00.)

I then did a full MemTest86 run which came back clear. Finally, I connected up
the rest of my hardware and powered on the motherboard again. I checked that all
hard drives were detected before proceeding to boot. All the drives were showing
in the BMC web interface, so I let the motherboard continue booting, and TrueNAS
started like nothing had ever happened.

### Other notes

The time from power-on to GRUB appearing for a warm start is 67 seconds. Idle
power consumption in TrueNAS is around 55 to 58W. That’s with three case fans,
one CPU fan, four SATA HDDs, one SATA SSD and one PCIe 10 GbE card. (Running any
TrueNAS apps or similar with continuous activity will push up power consumption,
of course.)

I’ve set power limit 1 (PL1) and power limit 2 (PL2) on the CPU to 65W in the
BIOS to keep power usage down under load (though for the most part it will be
nowhere near that).

### Wrapping up

Okay, this wasn’t the most exciting subject. But it’s nice to have a more modern
motherboard and CPU in my trusty NAS box. I had my old motherboard and CPU for
seven years, so let’s see how long this one sticks.

I find it convenient to add gift[^1] and loyalty cards to Google Wallet on my
Android phone. It keeps them in one quick-to-open place, often with
self-updating gift card balances too.

But one recurring bit of pain is that I often find it difficult to get
self-service tills to scan barcodes shown from Google Wallet. And holding your
phone at endless different angles in the hope that the barcode will scan, while
someone is waiting behind you, is a good way to make you feel flustered and
incompetent. Often even that doesn’t work, and you resort to scanning the
barcode from somewhere else, like the retailer’s own app or a print-out, or you
just give up.

I’ve only really noticed this problem at the compact type of self-service till
you’d often find at a Tesco Express, for example (and incidentally, a Tesco
Clubcard is one of the things that gives me problems). Larger self-service tills
like those at a Tesco superstore are usually no problem.

I never really understood what the problem is – I thought maybe the scanner
doesn’t like phone displays or my screen protector, or perhaps the clear screen
in front of the scanner was in need of a clean. But none of those reasons would
explain why scanning the barcode from somewhere else on my phone often works.

I got a bit of a clue about what the problem might be when I had a particular
e-gift card that wouldn’t scan when shown in my web browser. That was in
portrait mode – I noticed that the gift card would scan fine if I put my phone
in landscape mode. Switching to landscape mode both enlarged the
(one-dimensional) barcode and added more white space around it, and so I assumed
one or both of those is what did the trick.

It was after discovering that I realised that you can tap barcodes in Google
Wallet to show them on a pure white screen (usually with the barcode a bit
bigger too). Here’s an example of the difference[^2]:

And barcodes are indeed far easier to scan in the enlarged view (though it still
might take holding your phone at a funny angle).

I’ve probably tapped a barcode in Google Wallet and seen that view before at
some point in the past. But it’s such a non-obvious interaction (and you have to
tap on or close to the barcode itself, rather than somewhere else in the larger
coloured box), it had never occurred to me to try tapping there when I was
having difficulty getting a pass to scan.

There’s plenty of space for Google to add a ‘Pass not scanning?’ or ‘Expand’
link in the normal view to get to the big-white-background view, which would
make it far more obvious that it exists (and would’ve helped me out much
earlier). (Though it’d be good to better optimise the normal view for ease of
scanning in the first place – tapping an extra time to get to the enlarged view
and then having to go back two screens to get to a payment card afterwards is a
bit cumbersome.)

(Another thing worth mentioning is that if you switch your phone to landscape
mode before opening a pass, the pass will also open in landscape mode. Once the
pass has been opened, you can’t switch between portrait and landscape modes.
This may be deliberate to avoid problems when holding your phone at funny angles
with auto-rotation on. But I have auto-rotation off and normally only use manual
rotation, and I never knew landscape mode was supported until now. I had
previously wanted to try landscape mode in Google Wallet to see if it helps with
getting 1D barcodes to scan in particular.)

## Barcode problems elsewhere

Dark mode is also something that can cause problems with barcodes. In Gmail, for
example, the background of the barcode can get messed up. (In the most recent
example I’ve had, the email used an HTML table to create the barcode, with an
explicit white background on the table. The Gmail app changed the background
colour of the barcode, rendering it almost invisible.) Naturally, this can be
worked around by temporarily disabling dark mode (for example, using a quick
settings tile).

Web browsers that have forced dark theming for web pages can also break
barcodes. Even if the background of the barcode itself stays white, if the
surrounding area turns black with no border around the barcode, the left and
right edges of the barcode will be hard to distinguish from the background.
Similar to the Gmail problem, this is easily defeated by temporarily disabling
forced dark theming in the browser – but you have to know that you need to do
that.
